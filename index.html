The Market
1. Market Size

Analyst estimates for the observability market vary by scope. Narrow definitions covering observability tools and platforms cluster around $2–5B. Broader definitions that include adjacent infrastructure monitoring reach higher. All agree on double-digit growth.

$5.4B by 2030, 10.7% CAGR. Broader scope including adjacent monitoring categories.
Gartner
12% CAGR through 2027 for the observability sector within infrastructure software.

Growth is driven by cloud-native complexity, Kubernetes adoption, the proliferation of microservices, and the emerging requirement to monitor AI workloads. Observability spend has become a top-three cloud cost line item for many enterprises, which simultaneously fuels demand and creates pricing sensitivity – a tension visible across the vendor landscape.

OpenTelemetry
5. The OpenTelemetry Project

OpenTelemetry is the second-highest-velocity project in the Cloud Native Computing Foundation, behind only Kubernetes. Formed in 2019 from the merger of Google’s OpenCensus and the CNCF’s OpenTracing, OTel defines a vendor-neutral standard for generating, collecting, and transmitting telemetry data – traces, metrics, and logs.

The project reports contributions from over 10,000 individuals across 1,200 companies. Approximately 900 developers from 200 companies contribute monthly – an 18% year-over-year increase in developer participation and a 22% rise in company involvement. The top organizational contributors – Splunk, Microsoft, Lightstep (now ServiceNow), Google, and Dynatrace – account for roughly 60% of contributions. The remaining 40% comes from startups, individual contributors, and smaller vendors.

6. Signal Maturity

OTel organizes telemetry into signals, each at a different stage of maturity. The three core signals – traces, metrics, and logs – have reached stable or GA status across APIs, SDKs, and the OTLP wire protocol. Two newer signals are in earlier stages.

Traces
Stable. The most mature signal, with broad production adoption since 2021–2022.
Metrics
Stable. GA across major language SDKs (Java, .NET, Python, Go, JavaScript).
Logs
Stable. The Logs Bridge API connects existing log frameworks (Log4j, SLF4J, Python logging) into the OTel ecosystem.
Profiling
Public Alpha (March 2026). Uses eBPF-based agents. Cross-signal correlation with traces via span IDs is in development. Based in part on technology donated by Elastic.
Events
Experimental. Structured, named log records with well-defined semantics, built as a layer on top of logs.

Competitive Dynamics
8. Collection Commoditization

OpenTelemetry’s core structural effect is the commoditization of telemetry collection. Before OTel, instrumenting an application with one vendor’s agent meant telemetry was in that vendor’s format, sent to that vendor’s endpoints. Switching vendors meant re-instrumenting everything – a tax that functioned as lock-in.

With OTLP as a de facto wire-format standard, organizations instrument once and can route data to any compatible backend by changing an exporter configuration. The OTel Collector can fan data to multiple backends simultaneously, enabling parallel evaluations and gradual migrations. Switching costs drop from months of re-instrumentation to days of backend configuration.

This shifts vendor differentiation from how you collect data to what you do with it: analytics, AI-driven automation, query performance, visualization, and platform breadth. Vendors can no longer rely on instrumentation lock-in as a moat.

9. Vendor Positioning

Each major vendor has adopted a distinct posture toward OTel, reflecting their architectural history and business model.

Datadog
Embrace and extend. Accepts OTLP via the Datadog Agent and a direct intake API. Launched the Datadog Distribution of the OTel Collector (DDOT) at DASH 2025. Supports OTel-native metrics in dashboards via a Semantic Mode that combines OTel and Datadog data. But the proprietary agent remains the recommended path – it is the upsell surface for network monitoring, security, profiling, and database monitoring. OTel threatens the agent’s centrality.
Dynatrace
Governance leader and hybrid model. Contributes to the OTel Technical Committee, specifications, and Collector. Ships a production-ready, security-patched OTel Collector distribution for free. Grail data lakehouse stores OTel data natively without lossy translation. DQL operates directly on OTel attributes. The OneAgent + OTel hybrid positions OTel as complementary to automatic instrumentation.
Grafana Labs
Structurally aligned. The open-source backend stack – Tempo (traces), Mimir (metrics), Loki (logs), Pyroscope (profiling) – accepts OTLP natively. Grafana Alloy (formerly Grafana Agent) is built on the OTel Collector framework. As collection commoditizes, Grafana becomes the default open-source backend.
Honeycomb
OTel-native from inception. Co-founded by OTel contributors. The strongest OTel-native query experience, but niche revenue (~$10.8M).
Splunk / Cisco
Deep OTel roots via the SignalFx acquisition (SignalFx founders co-created OTel). Splunk Observability Cloud is OTLP-native. Post-Cisco integration has created organizational uncertainty.

10. Cloud Provider Strategies

All three major cloud providers now support OTLP ingestion natively, each with a different strategic calculus.

Google Cloud
Instrumental in creating OTel (via the OpenCensus/OpenTracing merger). Enabled native OTLP ingestion in Cloud Trace via the telemetry.googleapis.com endpoint. Strategic interest: OTel reduces switching costs, helping Google compete for workloads that might otherwise be locked into AWS-specific tooling.
Microsoft Azure
Recommends the Azure Monitor OpenTelemetry Distro as the primary instrumentation path. Actively migrating customers from legacy Application Insights SDKs. Deep investment in .NET OTel SDK. Added AI agent monitoring to Application Insights with OTel collection from Microsoft Foundry, Copilot Studio, LangChain, and OpenAI Agents SDK.
AWS
Ships the AWS Distro for OpenTelemetry (ADOT). Built CloudWatch Application Signals – APM-like service maps, SLO monitoring, and correlated traces/metrics/logs – on top of OTel data. Strategy: “good enough, deeply integrated, and included.”

For third-party vendors, this is a double-edged dynamic. It validates OTel as the standard and makes multi-cloud instrumentation practical – positive for platforms like Datadog that serve cross-cloud environments. But it enables “good enough” built-in observability that compresses the low end of the addressable market. The marketplace channel (Datadog is a top seller on AWS Marketplace, with purchases counting toward committed cloud spend) provides commercial cushion but also creates platform dependency.

11. OTel Beyond APM

OTel’s expansion beyond application tracing creates new competitive vectors against entrenched infrastructure monitoring vendors.

Kubernetes
The OTel Operator manages Collectors and auto-instrumentation via Kubernetes-native CRDs. Supports DaemonSet, sidecar, and deployment patterns. Receivers for kubelet stats, cluster state, and k8s objects provide infrastructure telemetry without proprietary agents.
Infrastructure
The host metrics receiver collects CPU, memory, disk, and network. Integration breadth still lags proprietary agents (Datadog’s 750+ integrations vs. ~100+ OTel receivers), but the gap narrows each release.
Fleet Management
OpAMP (Open Agent Management Protocol) enables remote configuration, health monitoring, and updates for Collector fleets at scale. Nike presented an enterprise-scale OpAMP implementation at KubeCon North America 2025. Once mature, OpAMP makes OTel Collectors as operationally manageable as proprietary agents – removing a key barrier to enterprise adoption.

Architectural Implications
12. Data Model Translation

OTel uses a hierarchical attribute model with three layers: resource attributes describing the entity (service, host, container), scope attributes identifying the instrumentation library, and signal-level attributes on individual spans, metrics, or logs. Attributes use dot-notation semantic conventions:

http.request.method service.name k8s.pod.name http.response.status_code deployment.environment.name gen_ai.request.model

Vendors with flat tag models must collapse this hierarchy. Datadog’s key:value tags lose the resource/span distinction entirely – a service.name resource attribute and an http.request.method span attribute both become flat tags. Datadog maintains a documented semantic mapping layer for translation, but the mapping is lossy by design. Typed OTel attributes (int, double, bool, array) are coerced to strings. Resource identity – a first-class concept in OTel – does not exist in Datadog’s data model.

Vendors with schema-on-read backends preserve the hierarchy natively. Dynatrace’s Grail and Grafana’s Tempo store OTel data without translation, making resource. vs. span. distinction queryable.

13. Query Fragmentation

There is no OTel-standard query language; OTel explicitly scopes itself to telemetry generation, collection, and transport. Each vendor has filled this gap differently, and the degree to which a vendor’s query experience is OTel-native is becoming a meaningful axis of differentiation.

Datadog
Multiple syntaxes per signal: a DSL for metrics (avg:metric{tag:val} by {group}), Lucene-style search for logs and traces (@attribute:value), and the newer DDSQL (SQL dialect with tag support). A “Semantic Mode” can combine equivalent OTel and Datadog metrics automatically.
Dynatrace
DQL (Dynatrace Query Language): a single pipe-based language across all signals. Operates directly on OTel attributes with no translation. fetch spans | filter http.response.status_code >= 500
Grafana
Signal-specific languages: PromQL (metrics), LogQL (logs), TraceQL (traces). TraceQL was designed expressly for OTel’s span model – it supports resource. vs. span. distinction and structural parent/child queries.
Honeycomb
Visual query builder operating directly on raw events. Fully OTel-native, designed around OTel’s wide-event attribute model.

An OTel-native query experience would support: dot-notation attributes as first-class citizens, typed values without facet declaration, resource/span separation, semantic convention version awareness, and structural trace queries. No vendor fully delivers all of these today, but Dynatrace (DQL) and Grafana (TraceQL) are closest.

OTel and AI
14. OTel GenAI Conventions

OpenTelemetry has published semantic conventions for generative AI systems, covering model spans, agent spans, events, and metrics. Technology-specific conventions exist for Anthropic, OpenAI, Azure AI Inference, and AWS Bedrock.

gen_ai.request.model gen_ai.usage.input_tokens gen_ai.usage.output_tokens gen_ai.response.finish_reasons gen_ai.operation.name gen_ai.system

Work on agent framework conventions – covering CrewAI, AutoGen, LangGraph, IBM Bee Stack, and others – is ongoing. The community is defining a standardized approach for tracing multi-step tool use, retrieval-augmented generation, and multi-agent orchestration. Datadog natively supports OTel GenAI Semantic Conventions (v1.37+), automatically mapping GenAI attributes to its LLM Observability schema.

If these conventions achieve the same adoption trajectory as OTel’s HTTP or database conventions, they will standardize AI workload telemetry across providers and frameworks – repeating the pattern that transformed APM from a proprietary-instrumentation market to an open-standards one.

15. AI for Observability

Every major vendor now ships AI-assisted features for anomaly detection, root cause analysis, natural language querying, and automated remediation. The 2025 Gartner MQ noted AI capabilities as a significant evaluation criterion. Two distinct architectural approaches have emerged.

Datadog
Bits AI suite (2025): SRE Agent (autonomous incident troubleshooting), Dev Agent (development-time assistance), Security Analyst. LLM-driven, correlation-based. In 2026, launched an MCP Server enabling coding agents (Claude Code, Cursor, Codex) to access Datadog telemetry.
Dynatrace
Davis AI: causal, topology-aware reasoning. Uses the Smartscape dependency graph for root cause analysis rather than pure correlation. Longer track record in production AI for observability.
Cisco / Splunk
Announced agentic AI-powered Splunk Observability in September 2025. AI agents deployed across the full incident response lifecycle.
Microsoft
Added AI agent monitoring to Application Insights with OTel-collected telemetry from Foundry, Copilot Studio, LangChain, and OpenAI Agents SDK.

16. Observability for AI

The inverse of AI for observability: monitoring AI/ML workloads themselves. This encompasses LLM inference latency, token costs, hallucination rates, GPU utilization, model drift, and AI agent execution traces. This is the category driving the highest growth rates in the observability market.

Datadog’s LLM Observability product (GA) provides end-to-end tracing across AI agents with visibility into inputs, outputs, latency, token usage, and errors. The June 2025 expansion added agentic AI monitoring, LLM Experiments, and an AI Agents Console. A Q4 2025 eight-figure deal with “a leading AI model company” signals that AI infrastructure operators are emerging as a material revenue segment.

OTel’s GenAI conventions position it as the neutral instrumentation layer for these workloads. As AI agent architectures grow more complex, the need for standardized tracing across heterogeneous frameworks strengthens the case for OTel as the common telemetry layer.

19. OTel-Native Startups

A cohort of startups has been built from the ground up on OpenTelemetry, with no proprietary agent or instrumentation format. Their existence is a direct consequence of OTel’s commoditization of the collection layer – they compete entirely on backend value.

$1B valuation, $155M total funding (Series B $110M, March 2026, Balderton). Founded 2023 by the team behind Instana (acquired by IBM, 2020). 600+ paying customers including Zalando and Taco Bell, more than doubling in five months between rounds. Acquired Lumigo. 21. Telemetry Pipelines

Cribl is the dominant independent telemetry pipeline company, positioned at the strategic chokepoint between data producers and observability backends.

Cribl Stream routes, filters, and transforms observability data between sources and destinations. The value proposition: enterprises reduce Splunk and Datadog spend by 30–90% by processing data before it reaches the backend. Cribl closed a $319M Series E led by GV (Google Ventures) in August 2024 at a $3.5B valuation. ARR reached $200M as of January 2025 – the fourth-fastest infrastructure company to reach $100M ARR.

At this scale, Cribl is more likely an IPO candidate than an acquisition target. The strategic significance of the pipeline layer – controlling data flow, reducing costs, enabling multi-vendor strategies – makes it a company that Datadog and Splunk compete against rather than one they would acquire.

23. Acquisition Patterns

Three patterns are visible across the startup and M&A landscape.

OTel-native is a prerequisite. Every funded startup in this space is built on OTel or at minimum OTLP-compatible. Proprietary-only instrumentation is no longer fundable for new entrants. The floor of the market has moved.

AI observability draws disproportionate capital. Arize ($131M), Braintrust ($800M valuation), and the Langfuse acquisition all reflect a bet on the AI workload monitoring opportunity. Capital is flowing in ahead of revenue, on the expectation that every enterprise deploying AI will need to observe it.

Incumbents are buying, not building. Datadog’s strategic investment in Arize, Snowflake’s $1B Observe acquisition, ClickHouse’s Langfuse acquisition, and Dash0’s Lumigo acquisition demonstrate that the build-vs-buy calculus has tipped toward buy for both AI and OTel-native capabilities. The velocity of the AI market and the architectural specificity of OTel-native backends make organic development too slow for companies that need these capabilities now.
